Musk is an enthusiast of meme culture—life is boring; a little fun does no harm.
The concept of meme was first introduced by British biologist Richard Dawkins in his 1976 book *The Selfish Gene*. He used "meme" to describe a unit of cultural transmission, analogous to genes in biological evolution. Dawkins defined memes as ideas, behaviors, or styles that spread through imitation—slogans, melodies, customs, and the like.
Simply put, meme is the "non-biological gene" of human society.
But precisely because it is non-biological, meme culture has a core problem: ambiguity and uncertainty. Most of the time you cannot tell whether something is a meme—it is highly context-dependent. The same phrase may be a meme in one group and ordinary content in another. (At a Hong Kong police event yesterday, most speeches were in Cantonese; guests were witty and the audience laughed often, but I could only catch about 10% of the jokes.) So your knowing smile may look foolish to someone else.
This ambiguity fuels creativity and inclusivity—anyone can reinterpret or create memes, driving cultural evolution. Yet memes can also be misunderstood, abused, or maliciously exploited (e.g., spreading misinformation or extremist views), often wrapped in humor and hard to take seriously.
Meme tokens may effectively be "entertainment assets with no utility value." As highly speculative crypto assets, their core appeal—viral spread and community hype—also makes them prone to scams, ponzi schemes, and gambling elements. These are not isolated incidents but may reflect structural flaws in the meme-token ecosystem: 99% of participants lose; a few whales or insiders profit.
In 2025, U.S. asset managers attempted to launch meme ETFs but were criticized as "casino-style speculation."
