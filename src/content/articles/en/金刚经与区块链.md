A monk asks Joshu: "Does blockchain have a master?"
Joshu: "No."
The monk: "Who validates transactions?"
Joshu: "Arising validates itself."
The monk: "What if 51% turns malicious?"
Joshu raises his cup: "The tea has gone cold."

Blockchain is modern engineering's best interpretation of "no authority, no sovereign"—translating Buddhist emptiness into cryptography and game theory.

Core Buddhist doctrines of "no authority" (anattā, non-self) and "no sovereign" (no god, no creator) hold that no phenomenon has a permanent ruler; all things arise and cease through conditions, equal and centerless.

Bitcoin and blockchain achieve a similar philosophy through decentralization: no single authority, no central sovereign—the network is maintained by consensus, not command. The two resonate deeply on "decentralized ontology."

Consider DAOs and what might be called a "sangha republic."

- Early Buddhist sangha: no pope; self-governance through precepts and consensus (Uposatha-day deliberation).
- DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization): token holders vote; no CEO; rules on-chain.
- Both embody leaderless collective intelligence, but share the risk of schism (sangha splits → eighteen schools; DAO forks → The DAO incident).
